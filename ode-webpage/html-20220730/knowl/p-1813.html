The ideal pendulum

\begin{align*} \frac{d \theta}{dt} & = v\\ \frac{dv}{dt} & = - \frac{g}{L} \sin \theta. \end{align*}

is Hamiltonian, since the total energy function

\begin{equation} H(\theta, v) = E(\theta, v) = \frac{1}{2} v^2 - \frac{g}{L} \cos \theta.\tag{5.2.2} \end{equation}

is the Hamiltonian function for the system. If we set \(g/L = 1\text{,}\) then the solution curves of the system are just the level curves of (5.2.2). The curves that join the equilibrium points correspond to the pendulum that rotates exactly to the top of the arc and then rotates back in the other direction.
