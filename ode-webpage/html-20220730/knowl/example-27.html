Example 2.2.10.

There is no reason why our nullclines should be limited to straight lines. The system

\begin{align*} x' & = y - x^2\\ y' & = x - 2 \end{align*}

has an \(x\)-nullcline \(y = x^2\) and a \(y\)-nullcline \(x = 2\text{.}\) These two nullclines intersect at \((2, 4)\text{,}\) and the system has a single equilibrium solution. The nullclines divide the plane into four basic regions (FigureÂ 2.2.11). By choosing a point in each of these regions and determining the direction of the slope field at that point, we can decide the direction of the slope field in the entire region.

The only equilibrium solution of our system occurs at \((2, 4)\text{.}\)
